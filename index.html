AI-Generated Music and Its Impact on the Music Industry

This project examines the rise of AI-generated music, how it is affecting streaming platforms and creator livelihoods, and where the legal and economic landscape is headed. The site is organized into five sections: a topic summary covering what AI music is and why it matters, data visualizations showing the scale of the problem, a blind listening test, an evaluation of the ten sources used in this research, and recommendations for anyone looking to find good information on this topic.

Topic Summary

AI music is music created by generative models rather than human musicians. These models are trained on large datasets of existing music and produce tracks that closely mimic the sound of human-made songs. Platforms like Suno and Udio allow users to instantly generate music from text prompts, and the output is increasingly difficult to distinguish from human work. A Deezer-cited study found that 97% of listeners across nine thousand people and eight countries could not tell the difference between AI-generated and human-made music.

The economic impact of these changes cannot be overstated. CISAC, which represents five million creators worldwide, projects that 24% of music creators' revenues are at risk by 2028 with a cumulative loss of 10 billion euros over five years. This is not just a legal or cultural issue but an economic one that potentially threatens the livelihood of musicians.

The Scale of AI Music on Streaming Platforms

The scale of AI-generated music on streaming platforms is tremendous and continues to grow. According to Deezer, AI-generated music went from 10% of daily uploads to over 34% during 2025, meaning over 50,000 AI tracks are uploaded to their platform every single day. Deezer is currently the only major streaming service that actively detects and tags AI content using a proprietary system, and they also remove AI tracks from algorithmic recommendations and filter fraudulent streams out of royalty payouts.

Spotify, however, relies on a voluntary disclosure standard where uploaders can choose to label their tracks as AI-generated. This is a virtually worthless filtering mechanism since there is no incentive for uploaders to self-report. Users on both Spotify's community forum and Reddit report AI-generated tracks showing up in their Discover Weekly playlists, with some claiming nearly a quarter of their recommendations are AI-made. The frustration is widespread enough that some users say they have switched to Deezer specifically because of its AI tagging system.

The Legal Landscape

The legal landscape around AI music is changing rapidly. The three major record labels (Sony, Universal, and Warner) sued Suno and Udio for training their models on copyrighted music without permission. Warner became the first major label to settle with Suno, with Suno agreeing to phase out its current models in favor of new ones trained on licensed content. This signals that the industry is moving toward licensing frameworks rather than trying to shut AI music down entirely.

Some solutions have been proposed. An AI royalty fund has been put forward that attempts to compensate displaced human creators, arguing there is a human rights obligation to do so. Another paper demonstrates that it is actually feasible to trace which copyrighted works were used to train a specific AI output, which could make royalty attribution possible in the future.

Recommendations for seeking information on this topic

If you are searching for information on AI music and its impact on the industry, you will find a lot of news articles and press releases but fewer academic sources. The issue is still young, so there hasn't been much time for research to be conducted. Also, the issue is important, new, and concerns something people feel very strongly about, which makes it perfect for news coverage. Here is what works best.

  • Academic sources are sparse but carry the most weight. The academic papers that do exist tend to be very dense and technical, especially anything dealing with copyright law or data attribution algorithms. They carry the most authority but require significant background knowledge to draw value from.
  • Industry publications are the best middle ground. Industry publications report on primary evidence like court filings and company data without the complexity of academic journals. They are the best balance of reliability and readability for this topic.
  • Platform newsrooms are underused. For platform-specific information, the newsrooms of streaming services upload valuable information relating to their policies. Deezer and Spotify both have newsrooms with official policy announcements that are often cited by news articles but rarely read directly.
  • Forums capture what formal sources miss. Reddit threads and forum posts are worth checking for the listener perspective since they capture frustration and honesty that formal sources miss. Do not treat them as evidence of anything beyond individual experience.
  • Be specific with your searches. Broad searches like "AI music" will return mostly opinion pieces and surface-level content. Narrowing your queries to specific aspects, like "Deezer AI detection system" or "Suno Warner settlement", gets you potentially valuable sources faster.
  • Avoid the technical rabbit hole. Avoid getting too deep into the technical side of how generative models work unless that is specifically what you are researching. Those sources can be very difficult to understand and thus hard to draw value from.
